📜 History of Barangay Manoc-Manoc

Malay, Aklan | Boracay Island, Western Visayas, Philippines

🐔 How Did It Get Its Name?

The name "Manoc-Manoc" comes from the local Aklanon word meaning "just like a chicken." This is because of a massive rock formation along the coast that, when covered with shrubs, looked like a giant chicken sitting on a sandbar. Even the Spanish colonizers noticed it — they called the place "Buenavista de Galle", meaning "good view of the chicken" — but the native name Manoc-Manoc stuck and is used to this day.

🕰️ Timeline of Manoc-Manoc

Pre-Spanish Era

The First Settlers

Boracay Island, including the area of Manoc-Manoc, was originally home to the Panay Bukidnon and Ati people (Negritos). The island was sometimes called the "Land of the Atis." Manoc-Manoc sits on the eastern coast of Boracay, facing the mainland of Panay Island. Its calm waters made it a natural shelter for traders traveling by sea on their swift vintas (native boats). Some natives who stopped to rest eventually stayed and became the barangay's first farmers and inhabitants.

Spanish Colonial Period (Late 1800s)

First Organized Barangay

During the later part of the Spanish period, Manoc-Manoc was temporarily organized as a barangay by Cabeza Etok (Aniceto Maming), who became its first Cabeza de Barangay del Barrio (Barangay Head). The Spanish also referred to it as "Buenavista de Galle", but the locals kept calling it Manoc-Manoc.

June 15, 1949

Municipality of Malay is Created

Through Republic Act No. 381, the Municipality of Malay was officially established. Manoc-Manoc became one of its recognized barangays, along with Balabag and Yapak — the three barangays that make up Boracay Island.

April 25, 1956

Aklan Becomes a Province

Aklan was established as an independent province, separating from Capiz. The Municipality of Malay, including Barangay Manoc-Manoc, became officially part of Aklan.

1960s – 1970s

Boracay Gains Attention

Boracay began attracting families from Panay Island who discovered its beautiful beaches. Before tourism, the island's economy relied mostly on farming, fishing, and copra (coconut) production. Manoc-Manoc was a quiet fishing community during this time.

1978

Boracay Introduced to the World

German writer Jens Peter published a book describing Boracay as "Paradise on Earth." This marked the beginning of the island's tourism boom. Manoc-Manoc and all of Boracay began to slowly transform from a quiet island into a growing tourist destination.

1990 – 2020

Rapid Population Growth

As tourism grew, so did Manoc-Manoc's population. From just 2,794 people in 1990, the barangay grew to 20,504 people by 2020 — an increase of over 17,000 people in just 30 years. By 2020, Manoc-Manoc made up 34.13% of the entire population of Malay, Aklan.

2005 – 2006

Boracay Declared a Special Tourism Zone

Boracay was declared a Special Tourism Zone in 2005. In 2006, the Philippine Tourism Authority (PTA) was given administrative control over the island, bringing more development and regulation to the area.

April – October 2018

The Boracay Closure & Rehabilitation

Due to serious environmental issues — especially an overloaded sewage system — President Rodrigo Duterte ordered a six-month closure of Boracay Island. The island was rehabilitated and modernized. Buildings were set back from the shoreline, vendors were regulated, and infrastructure was improved. In October 2018, Boracay, including Manoc-Manoc, reopened to tourists with new rules to protect the environment and control overcrowding.

Today

A Growing Community

Today, Barangay Manoc-Manoc is one of the most populated barangays in Malay, Aklan. It balances its role as part of the Philippines' most famous tourist island while still preserving its identity as a community of local fisherfolk and residents. Manoc-Manoc's coast remains a peaceful alternative to the busy White Beach — a place where history, culture, and natural beauty continue to coexist.
